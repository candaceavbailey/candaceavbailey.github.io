Influenza Staffing Plan Analysis

Exploratory analysis for a medical staffing agency that supplies hospitals and cinics with temporary support staff,
seeking to create a staffing plan for the upcoming influenza season.

Objective

As my second course project with CareerFoundry I took time to understand the fundamentals of data analysis and visualization.

I worked as the data analyst. The staffing plan, based on historical flu data and U.S. population sizes, was part of a CareerFoundry course. My tutor and mentor reviewed my analysis for accuracy and helped me interpret my statistical analysis to test hypothesises.


Analysis goals:

  • Determine when to send staff and hwo many to send to each state
  • Categorize states by staffing needs based on flu season trends from 2009 to 2017 and vulnerable population sizes

Profiled & Cleaned Data

  • Cleaned and checked data from the CDC and Census Bureau for consistency and relevance, removed suppressed data and formatted state columns to match across data sets
  • Created descriptive data profiles to detailing what data types were contained in each column.
  • Combined data sets to identify trends and quantify population sizes to compare against risk factors for fu relation complications.
  • Mapped key, secondary, and comparison data.

Initial Challenges

The CDC suppressed flu mortality reporting for states that reported fewer than ten (10) flu related deaths each year.

To work around this issue I recoded the suppressed values with the average flu death total, then converted the death column into a percentage. Showing mortality as a percentsge of the total population (in each state) made my analysis easier for stakeholders to interpret.

Statistical Analysis & Hypothesis Testing

Created a null hypothesis ("Flu deaths in states with larger populations are equal to or lower than smaller states.") and an alternate hypothesis ("States with higher populations will have more deaths from the flu.") to test the correlation between state population size(s) and flu mortality.

Summarized Initial Findings

  • Interim Stakeholder Report

Created visualization to show potential outliers and positive correlation between total population and flu related deaths from 2009 to 2017.

There is a positive correlation between population size and flu mortality rates. It can be assumed that because some states have a denser population of residents within the vulnerable population group (Age 65 and up) they will see more flu related deaths each year, but that's not where this plan should stop.

Seasonality, geographic access to health care, tourism and overall lifestyles differences should be considered when creating an adequate staffing plan to better serve states and cities with residents at higher risk for contracting complications related to the influenza virus.
